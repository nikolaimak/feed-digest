1. Introduction: The Blueprint of Modern AI

⏱ 0:00 – 1:00
Key idea: DeepMind has consistently treated AGI as a long-term scientific mission rather than a race to build profitable software. The current AI stack rests on four pillars — transformer-based, autoregressive, pre-trained, and generative — and DeepMind is actively working to move beyond all four.

For years, Google DeepMind has treated Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) as a long-term scientific mission — a quest to recreate the human mind, not merely a race to build a profitable software product. When the game of Go was considered essentially unsolvable for AI, DeepMind went all in on AlphaGo, investing heavily with no immediate return. That bet paid off spectacularly.

Right now, history is repeating itself. DeepMind is pouring resources into another seemingly intractable problem that nobody else wants to touch. Depending on how this plays out, they are either executing an extraordinary vision or making one of the biggest miscalculations in modern history.

To understand what DeepMind is building, we need to examine the blueprint of modern AI. The current paradigm is held up by four main pillars:

  1. Transformer-based architecture
  2. Autoregressive generation (next-token prediction)
  3. Pre-trained on massive datasets
  4. Generative output (producing new content)

Most researchers agree that this specific combination has enormous potential, but also has theoretical limitations that prevent it from reaching human-level intelligence. And that is exactly why DeepMind is already moving beyond it — systematically challenging each pillar with alternative approaches.

2. Replacing Transformers: From Attention to Selective Memory

⏱ 1:00 – 7:32
Key idea: DeepMind is actively exploring alternatives to the standard transformer architecture through projects like Griffin, Recurrent Gemma, Titans, and Gemma 4. These architectures address the transformer's quadratic scaling problem by introducing selective memory mechanisms — replacing the expensive "carry every page" approach with an evolving "index card" of compressed past information.

The Transformer's Power and Limitation

The transformer is a remarkably powerful idea. At its core, it consists of a stack of layers where each token repeatedly examines all other tokens in the sequence, determines which ones matter most, and uses that information to update its own meaning in context. This mechanism — called self-attention — works exceptionally well because it can dynamically decide how each token relates to every other token, making it very powerful for building contextual representations.

However, this power comes with a critical trade-off: it does not scale cheaply. As the sequence gets longer, every token must compare itself with every other token. The number of interactions grows roughly with the square of the sequence length (quadratic complexity), which becomes prohibitively expensive very quickly.

While modern systems still use attention, they rarely employ fully naive all-to-all attention. Instead, they adopt a growing range of efficient approximations, sparse patterns, and hybrid architectures to control the quadratic cost while preserving most of the modeling power. Most leading labs have largely avoided experimenting with entirely new foundations, but DeepMind is actively exploring alternatives and extensions, including recurrent and state-space inspired architectures.

Titans: Learning to Memorize at Test Time

In December 2024, Google released the influential Titans paper, titled "Learning to Memorize at Test Time."[1] The research team has since published follow-up work on nested learning[2], and more recently, Titans + Miras, focused on helping AI develop long-term memory.

The central idea behind Titans is elegantly simple: instead of remembering everything all the time, what if we develop a specific strategy to keep only the most important parts of the input?

Titans demonstrated that a model can be more selective about what it retains by using a surprise mechanism. The model is taught to memorize unexpected things — information that deviates from its predictions — and this selectivity made it more effective on common-sense reasoning, genomics, and time-series tasks.

This approach represents a promising direction for complementing or extending transformers, and it could even help with continual learning since part of the adaptation happens at inference time. So far, it is perhaps the most notable approach to challenge pure transformer architectures, and almost all of the research behind it still comes from DeepMind.

Griffin: The Index Card Architecture

In a lesser-known release, Google introduced Recurrent Gemma[3], built on the Griffin architecture[4]. Griffin mixes two key components: gated linear recurrences with local attention.

The difference between standard attention and Griffin can be understood through an analogy. Imagine you start reading a book:

Standard Transformer (Global Attention): When you reach page 2, you must tear out page 1 and keep it in front of you. By page 100, you are carrying 99 previous pages — an unbearable liability. This growing stack of pages represents the KV cache in standard attention.

Griffin: Instead of carrying every previous page, you maintain a single index card that is constantly rewritten for each new page. The model uses only local attention — roughly 2,000 tokens — to understand the context of words within the current page. The AI synthesizes the core meaning of the input into a fixed-size state.

The "gated" part is the most sophisticated element. The Griffin architecture relies on matrices called gate weights that learn the best strategies for retaining information during training. These gates might learn that bold words, specific numbers, or the start of a new chapter are more important and need to be remembered — along with far more complicated strategies for deciding what is worth keeping.

When something is decided to enter the index card, a recurrence gate looks at the past information and the new information and determines how best to integrate them. If the model learns the main character is tall on page 1 and has blue eyes on page 65, the index card will contain a unified representation of a character who is tall and has blue eyes. The index card is an evolving state of past information, not a list of items.

Gemma 4: Practical Efficiency at Scale

DeepMind then distilled the insights from these experiments into Gemma 4[5], a model designed for maximum compute and memory efficiency. Gemma 4 employs several key innovations:

  • Sparsity methods dramatically reduce effective model weight. For example, the "4 billion parameter" model actually contains about 25 billion parameters, but uses Mixture of Experts (MoE) and per-layer embedding methods to reduce the number of active parameters.
  • Hybrid attention: The model combines local sliding window attention for some layers with global attention for others.

Imagine reading a paragraph in a huge book — you first focus intensely on that single paragraph (local sliding window), then go back to read relevant pages to understand it in the full context of the book (global attention).

In a document of 256K tokens, some layers look only at a sliding window of ~1,000 tokens to ensure local coherence, while a limited number of other layers take the entire global context into account, connecting local understanding to the whole document.

These attempts might appear to be consumer products, but more than anything, they are public experiments serving DeepMind's core strategy toward AGI. As Demis Hassabis has stated:

"We need one or two more big breakthroughs before we'll get to AGI. And I think they're along the lines of things like continual learning, better memory, longer context windows — or perhaps more efficient context windows would be the right way to say it. Don't store everything, just store the important things. That would be a lot more efficient. That's what the brain does."

— Demis Hassabis, CEO of Google DeepMind

3. Beyond Autoregression: The Rise of Diffusion Language Models

⏱ 7:32 – 15:45
Key idea: Autoregression (next-token prediction) is not inherent to transformers — it was an architectural choice. Diffusion language models offer a compelling alternative: they generate text through parallel iterative refinement rather than sequential left-to-right prediction, offering advantages in speed, error correction, and flexibility.

Autoregression Is Not the Transformer

Right alongside the transformer is another fundamental idea called autoregression. The generation process in GPT-style models is next-token prediction — an iterative process where each new token is generated conditioned on all previous tokens. This process is so well-known that some people assume the transformer architecture is inherently an autoregressive system.

But this is a misconception. Even the original transformer, invented for machine translation, was not a pure autoregressive model. It was only when Alec Radford adapted it into a decoder-only transformer that it became fully autoregressive — transforming it from its original machine translation function into a more general self-supervised next-token predictor.

The main invention of the transformer was the self-attention mechanism, and that mechanism does not require next-token prediction or autoregression. This means there is an entire design space of models that are transformer-based but not autoregressive.

How Diffusion Language Models Work

Diffusion language models are the best example of non-autoregressive transformer models. Professor Stefano Ermon, one of the pioneers of diffusion models, explains the core difference:

"All existing or most of the existing LLMs are autoregressive, meaning that they generate text or code one word, one token at a time, left to right. On the other hand, some of the best generative models for images, video, and music are diffusion-based, where the object is generated by an iterative refinement process — you start with a rough guess of what the answer should be and then keep refining it. Crucially, this refinement process is highly parallel."

— Stefano Ermon, Stanford / Inception Labs

Three Advantages of Diffusion Models

1. Speed and Efficiency. Although diffusion models also loop, they typically need far fewer iterations to reach a final answer because the process is massively parallel — which GPUs excel at. A reasonable number of steps for language generation is often in the tens or at most low hundreds, as opposed to thousands or even tens of thousands of sequential steps for autoregressive models. In practice, diffusion language models are already approximately 10× faster.

2. Smarter Outputs with Built-in Error Correction. Autoregressive LLMs generate text left to right — once a token is generated, there is no opportunity to revise it. Diffusion models, on the other hand, can more naturally correct mistakes by working on the entire response holistically. The network is literally trained to fix mistakes, which is how the model learns at inference time.

3. Flexibility. While autoregressive models expect the prompt to be a prefix (everything before the generation point), diffusion LLMs allow the prompt to sit at any arbitrary position. This is particularly powerful for code editing: a diffusion model is naturally trained to see the entire codebase and complete the missing part, while autoregressive models struggle because they cannot naturally see what comes after the edit point.

Inception Labs and Gemini Diffusion

Although most major labs are ignoring diffusion models for language, a small lab out of Stanford called Inception Labs[6] has already introduced the first reasoning LLM powered by diffusion. It is dramatically faster than models of its size while remaining competitive in quality — the sort of leap that forces a reimagining of the entire stack.

Google DeepMind is the only major lab working on a diffusion language model, having introduced Gemini Diffusion in mid-2025. The jury is still out on the importance of diffusion language modeling — a hybrid approach might work best — but the direction is clearly promising.

OpenAI's Strategic Choice

The contrast with OpenAI is stark. OpenAI recently shut down Sora 2, its diffusion-based video model, letting go of a billion-dollar contract with Disney to double down on GPT technology. As OpenAI's leadership explained:

"The Sora models, which are incredible models, are a different branch of the tech tree than the core reasoning GPT series. They're just built in a very different way. And to some extent, we're really saying that pursuing both branches is very hard for us to do."

— OpenAI Leadership

DeepMind, by contrast, maintains projects across multiple stacks: VEO and Genie (video), Gemini Diffusion (language), the Imagen lineup (diffusion-based image generation), the Gemma family, AlphaFold/AlphaGenome, and many more. As Demis Hassabis puts it:

"This is one advantage we have of having such a deep and rich research bench. We can go after both of those things at maximum force — scaling up the current paradigms and ideas, and then really new blue-sky ideas for new architectures."

— Demis Hassabis

4. The Biggest Debate on AGI: Generative World Models vs. Joint Embedding

⏱ 15:45 – 26:53
Key idea: The most fascinating argument at the center of AI pits Demis Hassabis against Yann LeCun. Hassabis argues that generative video models force AI to develop implicit world models essential for AGI. LeCun counters with Joint Embedding Predictive Architectures (JEPA), arguing generative models waste compute on irrelevant details. The debate hinges on reconstruction vs. representation matching.

Hassabis: Foundation Models Are Essential

Demis Hassabis has been clear about his position on the path to AGI:

"It remains to be seen whether just scaling up existing ideas and technologies will be enough, or we need one or two more really big insightful innovations. If you were to push me, I would say I'm in the latter camp. But no matter what camp you're in, we're going to need large foundation models as the key component of the final AGI systems. Of that I'm sure."

— Demis Hassabis

LeCun: The Limits of Language

Many might automatically side with Hassabis because of his practical successes, but Yann LeCun's argument is deeply principled. As one of the foundational architects of modern deep learning, LeCun explains that self-supervised next-token prediction works for language because language itself is a constrained notation humans invented to communicate:

"In the case of text, that's a very simple problem because you only have a finite number of words in the dictionary. You can never predict exactly what word follows a sequence, but you can predict a probability distribution over all words. And that's good enough."

— Yann LeCun

The model has only about 50,000 to 100,000 possible tokens for output and can assign probabilities to them. But when it comes to something like video — a natural modality — training a model on self-supervised video frames is far more challenging because the number of possible frames for full HD video dwarfs the number of atoms in the observable universe.

"You can't do this with video. We do not know how to represent appropriate probability distributions over the set of all images or video frames. It's actually a mathematically intractable problem."

— Yann LeCun

The Latent Space Solution

However, this argument is somewhat of a straw man, because no one is trying to predict every pixel on screen. In a modern video diffusion model, researchers don't jump straight into predicting pixels. Instead, the process works in three stages:

  1. Encode: Use an encoder to move the image into a latent space — a compact representation that still allows for reconstruction.
  2. Diffuse: Train a diffusion model inside this frozen latent space, teaching it to denoise random noise into a valid latent representation.
  3. Decode: Convert the latent back into pixels.

The philosophical insight: instead of predicting inside the space of all possible future frames, modern video models exploit the fact that real-world videos occupy an extremely tiny, structured subset of all possible frames — constrained by physics, geometry, object permanence, lighting, and motion continuity.

Why Video Models Matter for AGI

But here is the trillion-dollar question: why would Google DeepMind invest so heavily just to create realistic-looking videos?

The answer is that this may be the clearest path we currently know toward AGI. The biggest flaw in current language models is a phenomenon called jagged intelligence: today's models can achieve gold medals in math olympiads and solve open mathematical conjectures, but still make absurd errors in basic physical reasoning.

The fundamental issue is that spoken and written language are just artificial artifacts of the human mind. Language is a super-lossy compression of our inner model of the world — it is not a fundamental part of human intelligence.

Consider the difference between someone who has read a thousand books on Formula 1 racing and Lewis Hamilton. The reader knows all the facts and physics, but the driver has developed such a deep, robust understanding that the car feels like an extension of his own body. The human mind relies on a wordless internal representation of reality — that is the core of intelligence.

This leads to the holy grail of modern AI: the world model. The first step to building one is teaching machines to predict basic physics across primary natural human modalities — vision, sound, and touch.

Hassabis' core argument is that generative AI video can force AI to develop a physics-based natural world model[7]:

"You can think of a video model that can generate 10 seconds, 20 seconds of a realistic scene — it's sort of a model of the physical world. Intuitive physics. It's intuitively understood how liquids and objects behave in the world. One way to exhibit understanding is to be able to generate it. That would be essential for AGI, because it would allow these systems to plan long-term in the real world."

— Demis Hassabis

LeCun's Counter: JEPA

LeCun argues that the generative approach is fundamentally limited because it is trained with the objective of reconstructing the original input. If the training objective is pixel-level reconstruction, then the model wastes compute predicting unpredictable details like the random movement of leaves on a tree.

Joint Embedding Predictive Architectures (JEPA)[8], which LeCun advocates, do something fundamentally different. The learning signal comes from pressure for the context and target encoders to agree on a similar latent representation, since both are derived from the same underlying scene.

AspectGenerative ModelsJEPA
ObjectiveReconstruct original input accuratelyPredict a compatible representation
Output spacePixel / token spaceEmbedding space
Compute focusAll details, including irrelevant onesOnly semantically meaningful representations

With video, the same principle applies: if a ball rolls behind a wall, JEPA must predict a compatible future representation where the ball emerges on the other side. Over enough examples, the model develops an implicit understanding of object permanence without ever generating the video itself.

DeepMind's Full Vision

If JEPA sounds so reasonable, why isn't everyone switching? Theoretically, architectures like JEPA should eventually outperform purely generative systems, but empirically, generative models still perform much better across many important domains today.

The key insight is that for DeepMind, the generative diffusion model is not the final product. The strategy is to build a hyperrealistic, grounded simulation of the world using generative diffusion models, then use that simulated world to train Gemini to develop an explicit, evolving model of reality internally. This is what Gemini Omni represents — not just another video generator, but an early iteration of DeepMind's world model.

5. Continual Learning vs. Large-Scale Pre-training

⏱ 26:53 – 31:08
Key idea: Researchers like Ilya Sutskever and Richard Sutton argue that massive pre-training assumes an inefficient learner and that true AGI requires continual learning from experience. DeepMind, however, bets that pre-training provides an essential foundation. The resolution will determine the shape of AGI.

The Case Against Large-Scale Pre-training

The final pillar of modern AI — large-scale pre-training as the foundation of the whole system — is one DeepMind has been a strong advocate for. But highly respected researchers like Ilya Sutskever and Richard Sutton suggest that this approach may be a dead end.

Their argument has two parts. First, learning from experience: the physical world is a messy environment full of unorganized goals, very subtle learning signals, and dynamic interactions. Performing all training in an isolated lab with rigid, arbitrary goals will never result in a truly intelligent system. The model should require only minimal pre-training paired with an extremely efficient learning algorithm.

Second, biological plausibility. As Sutskever explains:

"The thing that happened with AGI and pre-training is that in some sense they overshot the target. A human being is not an AGI — a human being lacks a huge amount of knowledge. Instead, we rely on continual learning. It's a process, as opposed to dropping a finished thing."

— Ilya Sutskever

The vision is not a finished mind that knows how to do every single job in the economy, but rather a mind which can learn to do any job. Once you have the learning algorithm, it gets deployed into the world the same way a human worker might join an organization.

The Richness of Human Learning

Most current learning systems operate with extremely rigid objectives: reach the target and receive a reward; fail and get punished. Learning becomes a harsh binary process.

Human learning works very differently. You do not need to crash the car to understand danger. The moment you sit behind the wheel, you already have a sense of what "optimal" should feel like — a sense of balance, control, confidence, uncertainty, and whether something feels wrong. The brain is constantly evaluating itself long before any catastrophic outcome occurs because the learning signals are so rich. Humans learn with astonishing efficiency and are surprisingly robust.

DeepMind's Counterargument

Despite these criticisms, DeepMind continues to advocate for large-scale pre-training. Hassabis argues:

"My betting would be that the quickest way to get to AGI is to use all the knowledge existing in the world right now — on the web — ingesting all of that information. I don't see why you wouldn't start with a model as a kind of prior to build on. My betting would be that the final AGI system will have these large multimodal models as part of the overall solution, but probably won't be enough on their own. You will need additional planning and search on top."

— Demis Hassabis

The Synthesis

This is the same philosophy that built AlphaGo. That breakthrough proved deep learning could solve near-infinite problems using deeply efficient pattern recognition. It gave DeepMind the conviction that AGI — despite seeming impossibly vast — can be solved using those same principles.

From one angle, Google DeepMind is doubling down on generative AI and massive pre-trained models. From another, they are actively pushing research far beyond standard transformers and autoregressive models. It is a highly specific bet on exactly what AGI should look like:

  • Architecture: Hybrid models combining transformers with selective memory (Titans, Griffin)
  • Generation: Both autoregressive and diffusion-based approaches in parallel
  • World understanding: Generative video as implicit world simulators
  • Foundation: Large-scale pre-training augmented by planning, search, and continual learning

Fortunately, it will only take a couple more years for us to understand who is right. What an odd time to be alive.
